The Aligned Life

How to stop performing your life and start actually living it. A journey from external achievement to internal fulfilment.

Sacred Exhaustion · Sacred Interruption · Rising Mercy~45 min · flexes 30–60Brave Plum Healing

Working script v2 for a ~45-minute keynote. Warm, grounded, fireside — a wise coach by the fire, never a sales floor. Built on Johnny's own sacred triad as the spine, in his exact words. Every your turn … is a real specific only Johnny can supply — nothing about him is invented. Read the 30-minute cut and 60-minute expansion notes near the end before you set your timing.

ThroughlineThe tiredness under a life that looks successful is not weakness — it is your soul carrying the distance between the life you perform and the truth you hold; and everything that keeps interrupting you is not punishment but mercy, refusing to give up until it carries you home to your essence.
The one big ideaFulfilment was never waiting on the far side of more achievement. It lives in alignment — when the outer life finally matches the inner truth. The gap between them generates a sacred exhaustion that scales with the distance; when that exhaustion can no longer hold itself up, a sacred interruption breaks in; and the mercy is in the escalation — feather, feather, rock, truck — grace refusing to give up until it returns you out of the performance, home to your essence.

The spine — Johnny's three sacred movements

The talk no longer teaches a technique. It walks the room through a mercy. The spine is Johnny's own sacred triad, used in his exact words and never redefined, softened, or diluted. (Notice · Name · Nudge is retired as the spine and survives only as a small toolkit inside Movement III — see Section 5.)

1. SACRED EXHAUSTION (Movement I — Sections 1, 2, 4)

Johnny, verbatim: "caused by the gap between the outer life and the inner truth. The further the gap, the greater the exhaustion."

The tiredness is not weakness and not ingratitude. It is the soul honestly carrying the distance. It scales with the gap. Never soften "sacred" to "burnout" or "stress."

2. SACRED INTERRUPTION (Movement II — Section 3)

Johnny, verbatim: "comes along when the sacred exhaustion can't hold itself up anymore." We tend to "play it down and dismiss it."

The structural pivot. The performed life can no longer bear its own weight, and something breaks in. Our dismissal of it is the hinge that sets up rising mercy.

3. RISING MERCY (Movement III — Sections 3 → 5 → 6 → 7)

Johnny, verbatim: "feather, feather, rock, truck: the more we dismiss it, the more aggressive the interruption becomes — first a feather, then another feather, then a rock, and then a truck. Rising mercy is what brings us out of the performance and returns us to our inner self, our essence."

The theological heart to protect: *the mercy is in the escalation* — grace keeps rising precisely because it refuses to give up on us. Not punishment. Not consequence. Love that will not stop knocking.

The arc in one line: a gap opens between outer life and inner truth → it generates sacred exhaustion (scales with the gap) → the exhaustion can't hold itself up, so a sacred interruption breaks in → we dismiss it → rising mercy escalates (feather, feather, rock, truck) → it returns us out of performance, home to our essence.

Section 1 — Cold open: the two versions of you

Target: 5 min

  • Walk to center. Don't reach for the mic. Let two full seconds of real stillness land before the first word — the silence is the first move.
  • Deliver the open, warm and unhurried: "I'm not going to ask you to answer this out loud. Just let it land. Somewhere in your life, right now, there is a version of you that you present to the world — the competent one, the one who has it handled, the one on the business card, in the meeting, at the dinner table. And somewhere quieter, there's the truth of how you actually feel underneath that."
  • Land the personal seam: "For a long stretch of my own life, those two things stopped matching — and no one could tell from the outside. That's the strange part. From the outside, everything looked like it was working."
  • RETIRE the old signature line "I call it the alignment gap." Name the distance sacredly, without yet defining the mechanism: "I want to talk about the distance between those two — the you the world sees, and the truth you carry. That distance is not a flaw in you. It's the most honest thing about you."
  • Plant the payoff line early: "The whole of the next forty-five minutes is really one gentle idea: you were never meant to perform your life. You were meant to live it."
  • Only now, in one unhurried breath, land who you are — by what you do, never numbers: a life coach, Reiki master, author, mediator, and retreat facilitator who guides people through the pivotal turns of their lives.
Speaker note: Make-or-break five minutes — warmth is your credential here, not authority. Breathe — the room needs to feel you were actually there.

Section 2 — The performed life, and the tiredness that isn't weakness (SACRED EXHAUSTION)

Target: 7 min · (Audience Moment 1 lands here)

  • Describe the performed life without judgment and without villains — no blaming "hustle culture" or "society." Compassion, not critique: how good we all get at building an impressive outside, until success quietly becomes a performance we can't set down.
  • Name the tiredness, then consecrate it. First the ordinary word for it — hollow, flat, quietly worn out even after the win. Then the reframe, delivered as relief: "That tiredness is not ingratitude. It's not weakness. It's not a character flaw."
  • Deliver Johnny's verbatim definition, slowly, as the heart of the section: sacred exhaustion is "caused by the gap between the outer life and the inner truth. The further the gap, the greater the exhaustion." Let "sacred" land — it is sacred because it is the soul's honest signal, not a malfunction.
  • The load-bearing nuance, in your own warm words: "This kind of tired doesn't come from doing too much. It comes from carrying a distance. It is your soul carrying the distance between the life you show and the truth you hold. That's why sleep doesn't touch it."
  • Hold both truths at once: you can have real, outward success and feel this exhaustion. Both are true at the same time — that's the whole confusion, and it's exactly why it gets misnamed for years.
  • Widen past careers so more of the room sees themselves: the relationship you keep the shape of, the version of yourself you present, the yeses you don't mean. Each one is a little more distance.
  • → Audience Moment 1 (silent, optional): "I'm going to ask you something, and no one will ever see your answer. Just let it land. Where in your life, right now, are you performing… instead of living?" Hold 20–30 seconds of real silence. Then, softly: "You don't have to do anything with that yet. Just notice — that tired place you just found? That's not you failing. That's the distance, being honest with you."
Speaker note: This is the "name the ache and give it dignity" movement — re-baptize the exhaustion the room walked in with, from "something is wrong with me" to "my soul is telling the truth." Widen from "me" to "us" with "maybe," "perhaps," "if this is you" — never "you all feel." Make it SAFE before you make it personal; that safety earns the first silence. Deliver the sacred-exhaustion definition verbatim and let it sit — do not paraphrase or soften "sacred" into "burnout" / "stress" / "fatigue." Absolutely no invented statistics about burnout or unhappiness; a plain human observation beats a number. If you ever want data for a corporate room, source it yourself and mark it verify. Protect the silence — do not rescue the room from it.

Section 3 — The turn: the interruption, the feather, and the mercy inside it (SACRED INTERRUPTION → RISING MERCY)

A wave of guilt rushing through. And then the line — "I forgive you for my wildness lost." (the line is Mike Beck's — credit him from the stage, every time.) "I knew in that moment that it was the beginning of the end of my educational career. How many students had I 'broken' so that they could fit in the box of our modern education system?" Deliver it in your own live words, not a recitation; the scene is yours. Twenty-five years as an educator, a superintendent's credential in hand — and a song about a horse opened the door. (Optional deep rhyme, use sparingly: "wildness lost" is the eagle in the coop — the book's title image — heard for the first time.) Anchor phrase, true to your story: "years of professional success that left me feeling hollow." Then name what it was, in Johnny's verbatim frame: "That was the moment the exhaustion couldn't hold itself up anymore. Something broke in. I didn't have a name for it then. I have one now — a sacred interruption comes along when the sacred exhaustion can't hold itself up anymore." Give this beat two or three full seconds of silence. No clinical labels, no dramatization.
  • The universal move — the hinge into rising mercy: "And here's what I did with it. What almost all of us do. I played it down. I dismissed it. I told myself it was a phase, that I was just tired, that I should be grateful, that I'd feel better after the next thing."

★ THE FEATHER → FEATHER → ROCK → TRUCK MOMENT — the single unforgettable beat

Build it slowly and physically, at fireside volume — each object its own beat, with real silence between. The escalation should feel like the room leaning in, never like being shouted at. Your hands can echo the escalation, small to large.

"The thing about that interruption is — it doesn't give up. The more I dismissed it, the louder it got. It starts so gently you can miss it. First… a feather." (pause, two seconds)
"You brush it off. You're busy. So it comes again — another feather." (pause)
"You explain that one away too. And because you keep your back turned, it can't stay a feather. So then… a rock." (pause)
"And if even the rock doesn't turn you around…" (pause) "…then, a truck." (hold)

THE REVEAL — drop the volume, do not raise it. This is what makes the image unforgettable and turns dread into comfort:

"For years I thought life was hitting me. That I was being punished. I had it exactly backwards. It was never punishment. It was mercy — getting louder only because I kept my back turned. Grace does not give up on you. It just keeps raising its voice until you finally turn around."

Then Johnny's verbatim rising-mercy payoff, delivered slowly:

"Rising mercy is what brings us out of the performance and returns us to our inner self, our essence."

Hold two full seconds before moving on.

  • Bridge to now in one humble breath: this became the work — sitting with people through the pivotal turns of their lives, with warmth and presence. No client counts, no numbers, no stories but your own.

Section 4 — The gap made visible: two open hands (the mechanism of the exhaustion)

Target: 5 min

  • A calmer, clarity beat after the peak — the recovery breath where you show the machine underneath what you just lived. Give the room the picture, not jargon: hold two open hands apart. "One hand is your outer life — what you've built, what people see. The other is your inner truth — what's actually real for you. The distance between them is the gap."
  • Make the mechanism explicit and tie it straight back to Section 2: "Remember that tiredness — the sacred exhaustion? This is where it comes from. It is caused by the gap between the outer life and the inner truth. The further the gap —" (widen the hands slowly) "— the greater the exhaustion. And as these two come closer —" (narrow the hands) "— the soul has less distance to carry. The exhaustion isn't random. It is exactly the size of this distance."
  • State the reframe that reorganizes the whole talk, and let it sit: "The goal was never a bigger outer life. A bigger outer life just moves this hand further away. The goal is to let these two come home to each other."
  • Redefine success generously, not cynically: achievement isn't the enemy — the distance is. You keep what's truly yours and set down what was only ever for show.
  • Callback to the cold open: "This is what it means to stop performing your life and start actually living it — not two hands held apart, performing for each other. Two hands that finally rest together."
  • Set up the practice as partnership, not more effort: "So the question the rest of us are left with is a very practical one — how do I answer the interruption while it's still a feather, instead of waiting for the truck? That's the next few minutes."
Speaker note: This is the "head" section — clarity over emotion, plain language; if a phrase sounds like a seminar, cut it. Keep the energy deliberately lower here; it's the exhale between the peak and the give.

Section 5 — Answering the feather: a small practice (RISING MERCY, made Monday-usable)

Target: 9 min · (Audience Moment 2 lands inside "Name")

  • Frame the whole section as working with rising mercy, not managing yourself — and keep Notice · Name · Nudge clearly subordinate: "The triad describes what your soul is already doing. Mercy is already rising for you; you don't have to manufacture it. The only real question is whether we keep dismissing it — or start to answer it while it's still gentle, while it's still a feather. Here are three small ways to answer the feather."
  • NOTICE (catch the feather): the interruption almost always speaks in the body before it speaks in words — a tightness before something you dread, a flatness after a "win," the small sigh behind a yes you didn't mean. Noticing is attention, not analysis; it is choosing not to play it down. your turn one small, un-dramatic "notice" moment from your own life — the quiet tell that something was off. "The feather isn't the enemy. It's the mercy, arriving softly, hoping soft will be enough." Bring the hands a little closer.
  • NAME (tell the truth under it): the interruption stays buried as long as it stays unspoken. Naming is saying the quiet true thing out loud, first just to yourself — "This isn't mine." "I've outgrown this." "I want something I've been afraid to want." "A feather you've named doesn't have to become a rock." Honor how hard this is; naming can cost something, and pretending it's easy would be dishonest — warmth here is staying beside them in the difficulty. Bring the hands closer still.
  • → Audience Moment 2 (private write, optional pair-share): "Take out anything to write on. In one sentence, just for you, finish this: The feather I've been dismissing is ______." Give 60–90 seconds. Then, softly: "If you'd like — and only if you'd like — turn to one person near you and share just as much as feels safe. If you'd rather keep it, keep it. Either is completely right." Bridge back: "You just did something most people spend years avoiding. You stopped dismissing it. You let the feather be a feather."
  • NUDGE (answer it with a half-inch) — defuse the fear directly: "Answering the mercy is NOT a leap off a cliff. You almost certainly don't need to quit everything or burn it down. A half-inch is a real answer." A nudge is one small, honest, reversible step that brings the outer hand a half-inch toward the inner one: one conversation, one boundary, one honored "no," one real "yes." your turn your first real "nudge" — the first small, ordinary-looking aligned step you actually took.
  • Show the compounding, gently: "Half-inches don't feel like much on a Tuesday. But they change the direction you're walking — and over a year, direction is everything. You don't leap home. You turn toward it, and take one honest step."
  • Land the whole thing as one warm line — your real, on-brand quote: "Real transformation doesn't happen to you. It happens through you — when you finally get out of your own way and let your truest self lead."
Speaker note: This is "the give" — it must feel doable, not homework-heavy. CRITICAL framing: Notice · Name · Nudge is NOT the spine anymore and must never feel like the real framework; it is a small toolkit that answers exactly one question the triad raises — "how do I stop dismissing the feather?" If in delivery it starts to feel like the main structure, shrink it: the sacred language leads, N·N·N serves. Every illustration in Notice and Nudge must be universal/hypothetical or drawn from YOUR own life — do NOT invent client stories or testimonials. Keep all body-awareness language to noticing and relaxation only — no medical, diagnostic, or Reiki healing claims on this stage, ever. For a formal corporate room keep Moment 2 fully private and skip the pair-share; for a retreat or festival, the share deepens it.
