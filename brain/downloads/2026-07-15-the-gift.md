## Verbatim (lightly punctuated from his dictation)

The most important question we can ask a young human is if they know what their gift is.
And I am not talking about the type of gift that is there for their own pleasure —
something to magnify their ego. I am talking about the kind of gift that their soul is
imbued with. The gift that was granted to them from the divine. The very purpose of their
existence. And we are given these gifts because they are intended to be shared with
humanity. Because we need them for the health of humankind. Gifts like those are not meant
to be hoarded or locked away because they are too threatening to be enacted, unleashed.

And yet how many young humans will tell you that they know what their gift is? I would
venture to say very few — and even those may have expectations confused with divine
purpose. And don't you think it makes sense? When children start into our public school
system, the very first thing that we do is begin to categorize, to catalog, to critique.
We assess the five-year-old's knowledge and begin to find the weakness — the chink in the
armor. And then the interventions begin, to shore up the deficiencies. Great effort is
taken to ensure that the child is "whole." More assessments are required, resulting in
additional interventions, and the child and their parents are now in the tempest of
self-doubt and publicly imposed insecurity. The child is not enough — who could ever
believe that they possessed some sort of magic when they are programmed to believe that
they are broken? The cycle doesn't stop, because the problem persists. It
self-perpetuates. Everyone has gone to school — so the madness is never questioned. The
self-doubt and the shuttering of the magic has become mainstream for generations now.

So if and when a child comes along that is an old soul, the system is not kind. It's the
tall poppy syndrome. The daffodils and the daisies will snuff the tall poppy out just for
standing above the rest.

So then my question is: when does the young human learn of their gift, and when does the
young human act upon their gift? I say probably never. How many people do you know that
are truly embodying their divine gift, intended to enrich humanity? Are you? Because when?
Not in school, not in the workplace, not when you are poor and stressed and anxious. We
are busy surviving and not thriving.

What would it look like if we were all expressing our gifts? And don't worry — remember
that the gift is granted because humanity needs it. There is no gift that is not worthy of
sharing. Your gift is precious, and so is mine.

I think that people exploring their gifts usually do it once they have finally had enough.
When the performance is too heavy to hold. When you finally realize that the life you have
been living is incongruent with the life gifted for you. Some may even say you are having
a midlife crisis, while I say no! You are in the midst of a **midlife transformation**.
And if you are one of the lucky ones that isn't waiting, and you are fully in the work of
exploring your gift — congratulations. You are an old soul, and you are giving us all
permission to do the same.

What could the world look like with you sharing your gift with all of us? Beautiful, I say.

  they are programmed to believe that they are broken?" Self-perpetuating; unquestioned

- **Tall poppy syndrome:** the old-soul child gets snuffed "just for standing above the
  rest" — daffodils and daisies vs. the tall poppy.

  TRANSFORMATION.* It begins "when the performance is too heavy to hold… when the life you
  have been living is incongruent with the life gifted for you."
- **The permission cascade:** the ones already doing the gift-work are old souls "giving
  us all permission to do the same" — (rhymes with Inside Out §6, the lit room).
- **The assurance:** "There is no gift that is not worthy of sharing. Your gift is
  precious and so is mine."
